Lesson No. 21

Waqf (Rules of Stopping in Quran Recitation)

(Page 48)
    1. Waqf means to pause or stop at a word or phrase while reciting the Quran.
    2. When making Waqf, you stop your voice and breath completely on the last letter of that phrase.
    3. Waqf does not change the meaning of the word, but it affects how it is pronounced.
    4. Rule 1: If the Last Letter Has a Harakah (Vowel Sign) Explanation: If the last letter of a word/phrase has any of these vowel signs: Example: Zabar (Fatha — َ ) Zayr (Kasra — ِ ) Paysh (Dhamma — ُ ) & Double Zabar (ً), Double Zayr (ٍ), or Double Paysh (ٌ) ,Vertical Zabar or Vertical Zayr Inverted Paysh Then, during Waqf, you should make the last letter Sakin (i.e., drop the vowel and stop with sukoon). Original word: يَرزُقُونَ in يَرزُقُونْ This means you stop without any vowel sound — just a quiet ending.
    5. Rule 2: If the Last Letter Has Double Zabar (ً) Explanation: When the last letter has Tanween Zabar (ً), then during Waqf, you must pronounce an "Alif" sound at the end. Example: Original: عَلِيمًا During Waqf: عَلِيمَا (with an "a" sound at the end).This rule only applies to Tanween Zabar — not Zayr or Paysh.
    6. Rule 3: If the Last Letter is a Rounded Letter like “ة” Explanation: If the last letter is rounded, such as: ة (Taa Marbuta) or هُ Then no matter what Harakah it carries, during Waqf it will be pronounced with a Sukoon. Original: رَحْمَةٌDuring Waqf: رَحْمَهْ This helps maintain proper pronunciation without vowel elongation.
    7. Rule 4: Letters That Do Not Change in Waqf Explanation: During Waqf, the following letters remain unchanged: Letters with Maddah (ٰا، ُو، ِي) Letters that are already Sakin (with Sukoon) Vertical Zabar remains as is Original: لِكُمْDuring Waqf: لِكُمْ (no change — already sakin).
